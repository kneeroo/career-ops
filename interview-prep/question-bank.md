# [SC / SE] Questions

## Your BIG Win — a complex / high-stakes deal

**Context:** At inforcer, I was supporting a mid-market deal worth around $25,000 ARR with an MSP planning to roll out our M365 security platform across their entire client base of 500 managed organisations. For a company our size, this was a significant deal. The complexity wasn't in the discovery or the demo — it was in the trial phase. During the POC, the customer identified a bug specific to how certain security policies behaved in the Australian region. It wasn't a showstopper for UK or US customers, but it directly impacted the workflow this MSP needed for their Australian client base. The deal was at risk of falling off entirely because the customer couldn't validate the solution with confidence.

**Actions:** I started by replicating the bug myself in a test environment to confirm it wasn't a configuration issue on the customer's side. Once confirmed, I documented the issue in detail — steps to reproduce, expected versus actual behaviour, and a clear assessment of what the fix would involve. I submitted it through our standard product feedback channel, but because the Australian region is a much smaller market, the bug didn't get prioritised. The customer was getting impatient. This is where the relationships I had built internally made the difference. I escalated directly to our Regional Vice President, walked him through the commercial impact, and made the case that the fix was simple relative to the revenue at stake. Because I had invested in building trust and credibility with leadership, the escalation was taken seriously. He took it to the executive leadership team, and they agreed to prioritise the fix. On the customer side, I kept communication transparent throughout — being honest about the timeline and making sure they knew we were advocating for them internally.

**Results:** The bug was fixed within the sprint, the customer completed their trial successfully, and the deal closed at the original $25,000 ARR value. Beyond the revenue, it became a proof point internally that the ANZ region needed a stronger voice in product prioritisation. The MSP went on to become one of our more engaged partners in the region.

**Learnings:** The biggest lesson was that relationship building — both internally and externally — is just as important as being technically strong. The internal relationships I had built gave me the confidence and credibility to escalate effectively, while the external relationship with the customer gave us the runway to fix the issue without losing the deal. I tried the standard channels first, documented everything clearly, and only escalated when I had a solid business case. I now apply this same approach whenever I hit a product blocker mid-deal: replicate it, document it, quantify the commercial impact, and escalate with a solution, not just a problem.

## AE Partnerships — and dealing with a difficult AE

**Context:** At inforcer, I was working with an AE on an MSP deal worth around $10K ARR. The AE was under pressure to close by month-end and had already promised the customer we could deliver a specific security policy integration that wasn't yet on our roadmap. I found out about the promise when the customer asked me about it on a technical deep-dive call.

**Actions:** My first instinct was to push back in the moment, but I held the line and took the question to the customer as "let me come back to you with specifics by end of week." Right after the call, I went to the AE privately and walked him through what had actually been promised versus what we could honestly deliver. I acknowledged the commercial pressure he was under and offered two alternatives: a scoped-down version of the integration we could ship within the quarter, or a phased approach committing to the full integration in a later release. I also offered to rewrite the customer-facing response together so the commitment was clear in writing and we were operating as one team.

**Results:** The AE initially pushed back, but agreed once he saw we had a path that kept the deal alive without over-promising. We sent the customer the revised scope the next day, the customer accepted it, and the deal closed that quarter at the original value. More importantly, the same AE started looping me in earlier on subsequent deals, and that relationship became one of my strongest internal partnerships.

**Learnings:** Pushing back on an AE is much easier when you come with a solution, not just a problem. The relationship is built in the private conversation after the customer call, not in the customer call itself. A customer-first attitude doesn't mean siding against your AE — it means giving them a way to keep the deal safe without compromising what you're delivering to the customer. Once you frame it that way, they take it every time.
