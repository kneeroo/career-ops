★ Hiltsje's brief — read this first

She told you the exam topics. Don't waste them.

"I think you're very well prepared for the interview already. Value-based selling as well as delivering value during onboarding is something we want to see throughout the interview process."

Two things to decode:

  • "Throughout" is the key word. These aren't two questions to answer once — they're the spine of every story you tell. Whatever she asks, your answer should land on either "I sold the outcome, not the feature" or "I proved value after go-live", ideally both.
  • She named onboarding deliberately. This is the hybrid pre+post-sales role. Most pre-sales candidates go quiet after the contract signs. The differentiator she's screening for is: do you stay until the customer is getting measurable value?
Win condition: Hiltsje leaves the call able to say "Niroo thinks in value and outcomes, in the deal AND after it." If she can say that, you pass.

1 The one thread to repeat

Say this in your opener. Circle back to it in your close. Let it be the line she remembers.

"Whether I'm in a deal or in an onboarding, the job is the same — find the business outcome that matters, prove it's achievable, then prove it actually happened."

That single sentence is value-based selling + onboarding value in one breath. It does three things:

  • "Find the outcome that matters" = discovery / value selling
  • "Prove it's achievable" = the demo / POC as evidence, not theatre
  • "Prove it actually happened" = onboarding, adoption, the measurable value loop (which is literally what Pendo instruments)

5 The bridge — tie the two themes together

This is what makes you sound senior instead of like two separate people.

Value-based selling and onboarding value aren't two skills — they're the same skill applied at two points in time. The promise you make in the deal is the promise you keep in onboarding.

"The risk in value-based selling is you over-promise an outcome to win the deal, then it falls apart in onboarding. The reason I care about owning both halves is that it keeps me honest in the sell — I only promise the value I know I can prove later. And then onboarding becomes the place I prove I was right."
This is the kind of answer that wins a hybrid pre+post role. It shows you understand why the role is hybrid in the first place.

6 CARL story bank — full spoken versions

Context · Actions · Results · Learnings. Each runs ~60-90 sec. Lead with the ★ stories; reach for whichever matches her theme. Drop a hard number early in every one.

★ Value-Based Selling — Melbourne Airport ROI

Use when: "Tell me about selling on business value rather than features."

Context: At UbiPark I owned the Melbourne Airport account, one of our largest enterprise deployments. The relationship had grown up at an operational level. The day-to-day parking and operations team used the platform heavily and liked it, but the executive layer above them had no real attachment to it. They saw it as a tool that worked, not a strategic investment. That's a quietly dangerous position for an account to be in, because when budgets get reviewed, anything that lives purely at the operational level and can't articulate executive value is the first thing exposed at renewal.

Actions: Rather than going back in with more features or a product update, I shifted the conversation to value. I pulled the platform's own analytics, the data we were already capturing on throughput, utilisation and operational efficiency, and reframed it into an ROI narrative in the executive's language. Not "here's what the platform does", but "here's what your operation is now able to do, and here's what that's worth". I translated usage data into dollars and operational outcomes a CFO or head of operations actually cares about, and I brought that story to the executive stakeholders directly instead of leaving it in the ops team. I was deliberate about leading with the outcome and using the platform only as the proof that the outcome was real.

Results: I got executive buy-in, and the relationship moved from "a tool we use" to "a platform we rely on". That repositioning was a direct contributor to the broader 20% retention lift I drove across the enterprise portfolio, because accounts with executive-level value attachment don't churn the way operational-only accounts do.

Learnings: Executives buy the outcome, not the dashboard. If you lead with the feature, you stay stuck in the ops team forever. If you lead with the dollar figure, you get the room. I now treat every demo and every account review as a chance to ask "what's the business outcome this proves" before I open the product, because the product is only ever the evidence, never the pitch.

★ Value During Onboarding — UbiPark Templates, 30% Faster Time-to-Value

Use when: "How do you make sure a customer realises value after they've signed?"

Context: When I joined UbiPark I owned enterprise onboarding end-to-end for customers like Monash Health, Charter Hall, Melbourne Airport and CBRE. The problem was that every onboarding was bespoke. We effectively rebuilt the wheel for each customer, which meant slow time-to-value and a lot of variance in quality depending on who happened to run the implementation. For an enterprise customer, a slow, inconsistent onboarding is the moment the value you promised in the sale starts to feel like it might not show up.

Actions: I made a deliberate decision to treat onboarding not as a setup checklist but as the phase where the value we sold actually gets proven. I mapped out what was common across our enterprise deployments versus what was genuinely customer-specific, then built reusable deployment templates for the common 80% and automated the repetitive workflow steps. I productised the playbook so it didn't live in my head, which meant the quality held even when I wasn't the one running it. Critically, I didn't just optimise for speed. I built in checkpoints around actual usage, because getting a customer "live" isn't the same as getting them value. I watched whether they were genuinely adopting the workflows, and if a deployment went quiet, I went in proactively rather than waiting for a problem to surface.

Results: The templated approach cut onboarding time by around 30% across the enterprise portfolio. But the number I care about more is the 20% retention lift, because that's the business outcome. Faster onboarding only matters if it translates into customers who stay and grow, and tying the two together is what made the retention gain stick.

Learnings: The first three implementations are research, everything after that should be templatised. And faster time-to-value isn't an efficiency metric, it's the value itself, the customer is getting what they paid for sooner. The deeper lesson is that onboarding is where the promise made in the sale either gets kept or quietly broken, so I treat it with the same rigour I'd treat a deal.

Adoption as the Leading Indicator — UbiPark Retention (the bridge story)

Use when: "How do you spot churn risk before the renewal conversation?" / when she probes the hybrid pre+post nature of the role.

Context: Across the UbiPark enterprise portfolio I was responsible for retention, and the trap with retention is that you only find out an account is unhealthy when you're already in the renewal conversation, which is far too late to do anything about it. I needed a way to see churn risk months before it became a renewal risk.

Actions: I built usage-driven engagement into how I ran every account, and I treated adoption as the leading indicator rather than relying on how happy a customer seemed in a quarterly check-in. A customer can tell you everything is fine in a QBR and still be quietly not using the thing they bought, and that gap is completely invisible if you only look at go-live dates and support tickets. So I watched the usage signal. If adoption dropped, I went in proactively to find out whether it was a training gap, a workflow mismatch, or a stakeholder change on their side, and I resolved it before it compounded. I was effectively doing customer success instrumentation by hand, stitching together incomplete usage data to build an early-warning system.

Results: That proactive, adoption-led approach was the engine behind the 20% retention lift. I was catching and resolving risk at week 4 instead of discovering it at month 6.

Learnings: Low usage in week four predicts churn at month six. Adoption is the single most honest signal you have about whether value is landing. The reason this matters for the Pendo role specifically is that I was doing all of this manually, chasing the signal across spreadsheets and partial data. Pendo is the product that instruments exactly what I was building by hand. So delivering value in onboarding isn't a new skill I'd have to learn here, it's the thing I've already been doing, finally with the right tooling underneath it.

The Pendo Flex — Lumen Health Instrumentation

Use when: "How are you using AI / hands-on technical work?" / "How would you ramp on Pendo?" — hold this for the technical/ramp question.

Context: I built a healthtech demo called Lumen Health, a patient-facing companion that translates clinical notes into readable summaries. The thing I actually wanted to prove with it wasn't the interface, it was the value loop. I wanted to know whether users were genuinely getting value, not just assume it because the app looked finished.

Actions: So I instrumented the whole product with Pendo, end to end. I installed it using the official AI Setup Assistant inside Claude Code, and in the process I hit and fixed the Next.js App Router page-load gotcha that breaks most naive Pendo installs, where the page-load events don't fire correctly because of how the App Router handles client-side navigation. I worked through it, got clean instrumentation firing, and ended up understanding not just how to install Pendo but the specific failure mode that trips up most first-time installers.

Results: I had working Pendo instrumentation proving the engagement loop on a production-deployed app, and a concrete, hands-on understanding of the product rather than a theoretical one.

Learnings: You can't claim you delivered value if you can't measure it, and that's exactly the worldview I'd been applying by hand at UbiPark. This is honestly the tool I'd have wanted back then. So when you ask how I'd ramp on Pendo, the real answer is I've already started. Show me an event firing and I can reverse-engineer the SDK from there, and I'd have a working sandbox before our next conversation.

Number anchors to keep front of mind: 50+ implementations · 100+ POCs · 30% faster onboarding · 20% retention lift · week 4 vs month 6. Drop at least one in the first two sentences of every story.

PRE-SALES (inforcer)

★ Complex Deal / Big Win — MSP Rollout, AU-Region Bug Escalation

Use when: "Walk me through a high-stakes deal you supported." Your strongest pure pre-sales story.

Context: At inforcer I supported a deal worth around $25K ARR with an MSP planning to roll our M365 security platform out across their entire base of 500 managed organisations. For a company our size that was a significant deal. The complexity wasn't the discovery or the demo, it was the trial. During the POC the customer hit a bug specific to how certain security policies behaved in the Australian region. It wasn't a problem for UK or US customers, but it directly broke the workflow this MSP needed for their Australian clients, and the deal was about to fall off because they couldn't validate the solution with confidence.

Actions: I replicated the bug myself to confirm it was a genuine product issue, not a config problem on their side, then documented it cleanly with steps to reproduce, expected versus actual behaviour, and an assessment of the fix. I raised it through the standard product channel, but because Australia is a small market next to the UK and US, it didn't get prioritised. With the deal stalling, I escalated directly to our Regional VP, walked him through the commercial impact, and made the case that the fix was simple relative to the revenue at stake. Because I'd built real credibility with leadership, the escalation landed. Throughout, I kept the customer in the loop and was honest about the timeline so they knew we were advocating for them internally.

Results: The fix shipped within the sprint, the customer completed the trial, and the deal closed at the full $25K ARR. Beyond the revenue, it became the internal proof point that the ANZ region needed a stronger voice in product prioritisation, and that MSP became one of our most engaged partners in the region.

Learnings: Relationship building, internal and external, matters as much as being technically strong. The internal trust gave me the credibility to escalate; the external transparency gave us the runway to fix it without losing the deal. My playbook now when I hit a product blocker mid-deal: replicate it, document it, quantify the commercial impact, and escalate with a solution, not just a problem.

★ AE Partnership — Holding the Line on an Over-Promise

Use when: "How do you partner with AEs?" / "Tell me about a difficult AE." Essential for a role that works deals alongside Account Directors.

Context: At inforcer I was working an MSP deal worth around $10K ARR with an AE under pressure to close by month-end. He'd already promised the customer a specific security policy integration that wasn't on our roadmap. I only found out when the customer asked me about it directly on a technical deep-dive call.

Actions: My instinct was to correct it in the moment, but I held the line and parked it with the customer as "let me come back to you with specifics by end of week." Straight after the call I went to the AE privately and walked him through what had been promised versus what we could honestly deliver. I led with a customer-first framing, that my goal wasn't to kill his deal but to protect the customer's outcome and our credibility, and I acknowledged the commercial pressure he was under. Then I gave him two real options: a scoped-down version we could ship that quarter, or a phased approach committing to the full integration in a later release. I offered to co-write the customer-facing response so the commitment was clear in writing and we showed up as one team.

Results: He pushed back at first but took the scoped-down path once he saw it kept the deal alive without over-promising. The customer accepted the revised scope the next day and the deal closed that quarter at full value. More importantly, that same AE started looping me in earlier on his deals, and it became one of my strongest internal partnerships.

Learnings: Pushing back on an AE is easy when you bring a solution instead of just a problem, and the real work happens in the private conversation after the customer call, not in front of the customer. A customer-first attitude doesn't mean siding against your AE, it means giving them a way to keep the deal safe without compromising delivery. Framed that way, they take it every time.

IMPLEMENTATION (Phocas)

Implementation Under Pressure — Contested MYOB Data Model

Use when: "Tell me about a difficult implementation" / "an onboarding that went sideways."

Context: At Phocas I led a BI implementation that included an ERP integration with MYOB, and the data model was contested. The client's IT team and our product had genuinely different views on how the data should be structured, and the whole implementation stalled on it. This was one of 50+ implementations I ran there, but it was the one where the blocker wasn't technical at all.

Actions: The technical answer was actually the easy part, I could see what the right data model looked like. The hard part was that this was a political problem, two groups defending their own positions, and you can't resolve that in a shared meeting where everyone's performing for their side. So I ran separate conversations with each stakeholder group, got alignment on the outcome we were all actually trying to reach, and only brought the technical solution back once that alignment existed, rather than leading with it and forcing a fight.

Results: We got the integration live and the implementation completed. It also reset the working relationship between the client's IT team and us, because they felt heard rather than overruled.

Learnings: In an implementation the blocker is rarely technical, it's alignment. If you treat a people problem like an engineering problem, you produce a technically correct solution that nobody adopts. I now read the room for who needs to be aligned before I bring the answer, not after.

Discovery-First Implementation — Reusable Templates, 25% Faster Lead Time

Use when: "Tell me about improving how something was done." Doubles as a value-selling AND reusable-playbook proof.

Context: At Phocas I delivered 50+ end-to-end BI implementations across mid-market and enterprise clients. Early on I noticed we were treating every implementation as fully bespoke and scoping loosely up front, which meant rework, scope creep, and inconsistent timelines across the consulting team.

Actions: I changed two things. First, I made discovery non-negotiable, running structured workshops with C-level and IT stakeholders to define the data requirements and lock scope before any build started, so we were solving the right problem rather than discovering it halfway through configuration. Second, I built reusable scoping processes and configuration templates from the patterns that repeated across implementations, and I shared them across the consulting team rather than keeping them to myself.

Results: The templates were adopted team-wide and cut implementation lead time by around 25%. Just as importantly, the quality became more consistent because the team was working from a shared, proven starting point instead of reinventing each engagement.

Learnings: Discovery-first is what protects the customer outcome, getting scope right before build is the cheapest time to get it right. And reusable artefacts compound where one-off heroics don't; the highest-leverage thing I did at Phocas wasn't any single implementation, it was making the next 40 faster for everyone.

Mapping to the hybrid role: the MSP big-win = pure pre-sales (POC + escalation + commercial close). AE partnership = works deals alongside sales. Phocas discovery/templates = value-selling + reusable playbooks. MYOB = "blocker is alignment, not tech" senior signal.

12 Things to avoid

  • Don't list features. The second you describe what a product does without saying what the customer gets, you've missed her brief.
  • Don't treat onboarding as setup. Never describe onboarding as "getting them live". It's "getting them value".
  • Don't answer the two themes as two separate boxes. Weave them. Use the bridge.
  • Don't under-sell the post-sales work. Your title says pre-sales; your UbiPark outcomes are post-sales. Claim them.
  • Don't over-explain the redundancy. One honest sentence, then pivot forward.
  • Don't forget to land the thread in your close. Last thing she hears should be the value reflex.
