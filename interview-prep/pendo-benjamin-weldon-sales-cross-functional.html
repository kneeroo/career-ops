Sales Cross-Functional — Benjamin Weldon

VP, Enterprise Sales, APAC · Senior Customer Engineer, ANZ · story answers use CARL: Context · Action · Result · Learning

Tell me about yourself.

"I'm a solutions / pre-sales engineer, five-plus years, all of it customer-facing and most of it in ANZ enterprise. I started in BI implementations at Phocas, 50-plus end-to-end, then owned enterprise onboarding and technical pre-sales at UbiPark for logos like Melbourne Airport and Monash Health, then went deep on pre-sales at inforcer in cloud security, 100-plus deep dives, demos and POCs partnering with AEs to win the technical decision. The thread through all of it is the same: I'm the engineer who makes the product real in front of the customer and then tells the AE what's actually blocking the deal. What pulled me to this role is that it's the full lifecycle in one seat, land and expand, which is exactly how I already think."

Partnering with AEs

Tell me about a time you partnered with an AE to close a complex deal.

ContextAt inforcer we were in a competitive bake-off against two established M365 security vendors for an MSP account. The technical eval was going well, but the deal stalled, the AE was getting silence on commercials, and the prospect's security lead kept raising new technical objections.

ActionI ran the POC against the prospect's own M365 tenant instead of a generic sandbox, so every objection could be tested live rather than argued. I tracked which objections survived a real test and which evaporated, and fed that straight to the AE: 'the security questions are resolved, what's left is procurement, not product.' The real blocker was the security lead needing internal cover to switch vendors, so I built him a one-page technical summary he could forward to his own exec. I stayed in my lane on price and handed the AE a clean, de-risked technical close.

ResultThe objection list collapsed once tested against the real tenant, and the deal moved from stalled to closed. The motion, test it live in their tenant then hand the AE a clean technical close, became the team's playbook for competitive bake-offs.

LearningA technical win doesn't close a deal, it just removes the technical reason not to buy. My job is to tell the AE which objections are real and which are cover, so they can sell into the actual blocker.

How do you collaborate with AEs and keep sales and technical aligned?

"Clear division of labour from kickoff. The AE owns the commercial relationship, the champion and the close plan; I own the technical proof and the technical buyers, and in an enterprise deal that's usually four or five people, not one. We run a standing sync so there are no surprises, and I treat it as my job to feed the AE risk signals from the eval early, 'their security team is the real blocker, not price.' The worst outcome is the engineer who technically wins the eval and then the AE gets ambushed on something I could have flagged a week earlier."

How do you handle a disagreement with the sales team on how to win a deal?

ContextOn that same inforcer deal, the AE wanted to keep answering the security lead's technical objections one by one. My read was different, I thought we'd already won on the tech and the objections were a smokescreen for a commercial or political concern.

ActionRather than argue my interpretation, I proposed we test the open objections live in the prospect's tenant, so the data would settle it instead of opinion.

ResultMost of the objections evaporated once tested, which proved the point without me having to win an argument. We pivoted together from 'answer every objection' to 'sell into the real blocker, which is procurement,' and the deal closed.

LearningI'd rather resolve a disagreement by running the test than by being the loudest in the room. Evidence keeps the relationship with the AE intact.

Tell me about a time an AE brought you into an unqualified deal.

"It happens, and I never make the AE feel bad for it, better to over-include me early. When I get pulled into something that smells unqualified, I don't run a full demo on faith. I run a tight discovery first to find the technical win condition, is there a real problem we solve, a real environment we can test in, an engaged technical champion? If two of those are missing, I'll say so directly and suggest we reframe the scope or refocus on a more qualified opportunity. Walking away from a bad POC early protects the AE's quarter and our credibility. My kill criteria: no clear technical win, no access to a real environment, no engaged champion, or a hard requirement the product genuinely can't meet."

Sales Focus

Walk me through how you approach a new opportunity, balancing technical vs sales objectives.

"Discovery first, always. I'm looking for the one or two things that have to be true for them to buy, the technical win criteria, not a feature checklist. Then I build the demo or POC to prove exactly those, and nothing else, because a POC that sprawls is a deal that dies. The balance with sales is constant: I map every technical proof point back to the business outcome the AE is selling, and I qualify hard early. At inforcer that discipline let us run tighter, personalised POCs and progress pipeline faster."

How do you make sure demos directly address the prospect's business challenges?

"I don't do generic demos. At inforcer, in competitive evals I built the demo environment using the prospect's own tenant configuration, so they saw the product solving their actual problem in their actual context, not a sandbox. It's more setup, but it wins the technical comparison every time because there's nothing to dismiss as 'that won't work for us.' That personalised-demo approach became a playbook the rest of the SE team adopted."

How did you contribute to the sales team beyond individual deals?

"Two ways. I build the reusable assets, the personalised-demo playbook at inforcer, the standard discovery-workshop format at Phocas, the onboarding templates at UbiPark that cut deployment time 30%. Those compound across every future deal, not just mine. And I run structured feedback loops back to Product: at two companies I aggregated field patterns into briefs that shipped features, which AEs used in renewal conversations as proof we listen. Individual deals are the day job; the leverage is in making the next ten deals faster."

Performance Consistency

What are the key achievements you're most proud of, and how do you measure success?

"Three I'd lead with. 100-plus technical deep dives, demos and POCs at inforcer where I owned the technical win partnering with AEs. A 30% cut in enterprise onboarding time at UbiPark and a 20% reduction in churn through usage-driven engagement. And 50-plus end-to-end BI implementations at Phocas. I measure success two ways: did the deal progress or close, and did the customer adopt and stay, because in a land-and-expand world the renewal is the scoreboard, not the signature. The churn number is the one I'm proudest of, retention is the hardest thing to move and it's the leading indicator of expansion."

Tell me about a time you exceeded expectations.

ContextMelbourne Airport had UbiPark deployed and running fine operationally, but the relationship had gone flat commercially. The exec team wasn't engaging at QBR, so no expansion was happening, and nobody had asked me to fix that.

ActionI reframed the operational dashboards into three financial views, revenue uplift, cost avoidance and risk reduction, onto a single one-page exec summary that mapped each feature to a dollar figure using the airport's own benchmarks. Then I walked their GM through it beforehand so he could co-present it as his result, not our pitch.

ResultThe QBR went from a status update to a strategic conversation, the account expanded 20% the next quarter, and it became a reference for two more enterprise prospects.

LearningExecutives buy the outcome, not the dashboard, and letting the customer co-present turns a vendor pitch into their internal win.

Have you faced a challenging technical problem during a sales cycle? How did you resolve it?

ContextA Phocas BI implementation had to integrate with the client's MYOB ERP, but the client's IT team and our product team flatly disagreed on the data model, the client wanted a flat feed, the product expected a star schema, and both sides escalated.

ActionThe technical fix was a staging-layer transformation that gave each side their own format. The harder part was political, so I ran three separate conversations rather than one joint meeting, because forcing either side to back down in front of the other would have blown it up.

ResultIt shipped on the original timeline, the client renewed and expanded, and the staging-layer pattern became our default for contested ERP integrations.

LearningSeparate the technical answer from the political one, they almost never get solved in the same room.

Adaptability & Coachability

Tell me about constructive feedback you received and how you responded.

ContextEarly in my pre-sales work I got feedback that my explanations ran too technical for non-technical buyers, I was answering the engineer in the room and losing the economic buyer who actually signs.

ActionI took it seriously and rebuilt how I present: lead with the business outcome and an analogy, and keep the deep technical detail in my back pocket for when someone asks for it.

ResultEngagement in my demos went up immediately, and it's now a core part of how I sell.

LearningRead who's in the room and pitch to the buyer who signs, not the one who's most fun to talk to.

How do you approach learning new technologies or sales methodologies the company introduces?

"I go all in and make it tangible. When I needed to learn Pendo I didn't read docs, I built an app and instrumented it. When I rebranded after a redundancy, I taught myself the full modern web stack and shipped production projects. I take ownership of the ramp rather than waiting to be trained, and I share what I learn. So if Pendo runs on a specific value-selling or qualification methodology, I'll learn it fast and actually apply it on live deals, not just nod through the enablement."

What's an area you're currently working to improve, and how?

"I'm deliberately sharpening the commercial edge of my game, formal qualification and value-selling frameworks like MEDDIC, and getting closer to the quota-carrying side of deals rather than just the technical win. My instinct for reading the commercial blocker is strong, but I want the structured methodology underneath it so it's repeatable, not just intuition. That's part of why this merged role appeals, it pushes me deeper into the full commercial lifecycle."

Technical Background & Sales Skills

Describe your technical background and how it relates to the role.

"I'm not a CS-degree developer who moved into sales, I'm an implementation and pre-sales engineer who can build. Two degrees from Monash, IT and business, then five years hands-on: 50-plus BI implementations at Phocas including data modelling and ERP integrations, enterprise onboarding at UbiPark with API integrations and SSO/SAML, and the full M365 security stack at inforcer, Entra, Defender, Sentinel, Purview, Intune. On top of that I build production software myself: Python, JavaScript, TypeScript, SQL, React and Next.js, I shipped a healthtech app and instrumented it with Pendo end-to-end. I'm technical enough to earn a customer's engineers' trust, and commercial enough to map it back to the deal. That's the bridge a customer engineer has to be."

Tell me about a time you had to demo under pressure.

ContextIn competitive M365 security evals at inforcer, demos ran live against the prospect's own tenant, which is high-pressure by design, there's no safety net and anything can misbehave on real data.

ActionWhen something didn't load the way I expected mid-demo, I never panicked or apologised into the floor. I narrated the problem-solving, 'while that loads, let me show you what this is actually doing and why it matters to your team,' and pivoted to the outcome.

ResultThe room kept its confidence, and because we were on their real tenant, the hiccup became a chance to show how we'd handle it in their environment, which built more trust than a flawless canned demo would have.

LearningThe audience remembers whether you stayed composed and in command far more than they remember the glitch.

How do you approach a client with a very specific technical requirement?

ContextAt Phocas a client needed our BI platform to integrate with their MYOB ERP, and their data model didn't match what the product expected.

ActionI started with a real needs assessment rather than a feature pitch, then designed a staging-layer transformation that gave them their format and the product its format, and I brought the right internal people in early instead of promising something I couldn't underwrite.

ResultThey were impressed because the answer was built around their constraint, not ours, and the pattern became our default for contested ERP integrations.

LearningAssess first, then engineer a tailored path, never force the customer into your shape.

Give an example of communicating complex value to a non-technical audience.

ContextAt Melbourne Airport the exec team wasn't engaging with the platform because they couldn't see the dollars behind the operational data.

ActionI reframed the dashboards into the three things a CFO cares about, revenue uplift, cost avoidance and risk reduction, on a single page mapped to their own benchmarks, with no feature talk.

ResultThe QBR turned strategic and the account expanded 20%.

Describe a challenging sales experience or a client unhappy with your proposed solution.

ContextAt UbiPark a set of customers pushed back that the platform lacked an analytics capability they needed, and were unhappy with the proposed path.

ActionI didn't over-promise the roadmap or get defensive. I gave them a workaround using existing features to solve the immediate pain, then aggregated the pattern across multiple accounts into a structured brief for Product.

ResultThe feature shipped the following quarter, and it turned a complaint into a renewal talking point because they saw we actually act on feedback.

LearningSolve the now with a workaround, be honest about the gap, and feed it into the roadmap through a real loop rather than an ad-hoc promise.
