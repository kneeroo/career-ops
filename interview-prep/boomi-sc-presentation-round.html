★ Read first — how this round is actually won

The brief, twice: "gauge your technical fit … and to assess your 'salesmanship' presentation skills." You "present your solution to the customer."

This is a role-play sales presentation, not a tech walkthrough. You are presenting to a customer (the panel plays the customer). The live Boomi build is your proof point, not the main act. The single biggest trap is over-engineering a slick integration and then presenting it like an engineer. Richard Yang already named the bar: articulate value and why/how throughout, not just demonstrate the tech.

The three things that win it

  • You sell, you do not narrate. Open on the customer's business pain, frame everything as outcomes (risk removed on $1M orders, CSRs who can finally answer "where's my order", nightly reporting that actually lands), close on value. The demo proves a claim you already made.
  • Your architecture is right, not just runnable. Every one of the seven requirements maps to a specific Boomi pattern (§2). The one that separates a real SC from a connector-dragger is the WMS maintenance window — answer it with decoupling and guaranteed delivery (§4).
  • You are visibly current. The deck they gave you is the legacy 2019-era platform overview. Layering the 2026 "Data Activation Company / control layer / Agentstudio / MCP" story (§8) on the bonus slide signals homework nobody else will show.
Time split in their heads: roughly 60% can-you-sell / 40% can-you-build. Plan your prep the opposite of your engineering instinct. A clean, confident pitch over a simple working demo beats a complex build presented flat.

4 The resilience play — your differentiator

Requirement 5 (WMS down 4 hrs/week, orders still arriving) is the question most candidates fumble. Artur's worldview is "integration is the core" — resilience is exactly the depth he rewards.

The naive answer is "the order fails and we retry." Wrong instinct: it couples order capture to a system you know is unavailable. The SC answer is decouple and guarantee delivery:

  1. SFDC order is captured and acknowledged immediately — capture never depends on the WMS being up.
  2. The order to the WMS is published to a queue (Boomi Event Streams or an Atom Queue), not pushed synchronously.
  3. A separate consumer process drains the queue and calls the WMS. During the 4-hour window the queue simply buffers; nothing is lost.
  4. On resume, the consumer works through the backlog automatically.
  5. Failures get retry with backoff, and anything that still fails lands in a dead-letter path that alerts an operator, rather than vanishing.
Say it like this: "Your warehouse goes down four hours a week, and that is fine. We never let order capture depend on the warehouse being awake. Salesforce confirms the order instantly, the order to the warehouse goes onto a queue, and the queue just holds the work until the warehouse is back. Nothing is dropped, nobody is babysitting it, and your sales team never stops selling during the maintenance window."
Same decoupling instinct also answers "what if Oracle is down when we try to create the order?" — queue or retry-with-alert, never silent failure. Reuse the pattern.

5 Security + assumptions (state them, do not hide them)

The brief explicitly says: document assumptions, talk through security. Doing it unprompted reads as senior.

Security story

  • On-prem access without opening the firewall: a local Atom runs inside the customer network and makes an outbound-only connection to the Boomi control plane. No inbound holes, no exposing Oracle / WMS / SQL Server to the internet.
  • Data residency: the control plane holds metadata only; actual order and customer data is processed by the local Atom and never has to leave the customer's environment unnecessarily. 256-bit encryption in transit.
  • Credentials: connection credentials are stored and managed in Boomi, not hard-coded in processes.

Assumptions to declare

  • Oracle E-Business is reachable via its DB / web services; WMS exposes the documented XML-over-HTTP endpoint.
  • In the live demo I use a Salesforce Opportunity (it has a native Amount field) as a stand-in for an order, and a database / file as a stand-in for Oracle and the WMS, because I do not have those live systems. The pattern is identical.
  • "Immediately" for order creation means low-latency real-time, not literally synchronous-or-fail.
The senior move: "Where I was not certain, I made an assumption and I will call it out as I go. In a real engagement these become discovery questions for your team." That sentence alone signals you have done real pre-sales.

6 What to build, and why

Full click-path is in boomi-sc-demo-build-guide.md. This card is the strategy behind it.

Brief says "one or more", "not production ready", content over format. Build one process end-to-end well, with visible logic, and talk through the rest on slides. Two builds, in this order:

Build A — your bulletproof baseline (do first)

CSV customer balances → validated → output (requirement 7). Flat-file profile in, a Cleanse / Decision step to validate records, a Map, then write to a database if you have one reachable, otherwise to a file or Return Documents. Add a Try/Catch that routes bad rows to an error file. This is trivial to make reliable and demonstrates batch + validation + error handling cleanly. It is your guarantee that something works live.

Build B — the headline (do second)

Salesforce order → Decision (>$1M) → map → create record + write-back. Query an Opportunity from your real SFDC Dev org, branch on Amount with a Decision shape, map the order, create a record in a DB / transform to the WMS XML, and write a confirmation back. This shows the actual business process (requirements 1 to 4) with the $1M routing front and centre.

Authenticity: use your real Salesforce Developer org as the live source. A real connector pulling real records is far more convincing than mock data.
Live-demo insurance: things break in front of people. Have Build A as the safe one, take screenshots / a short screen recording of both processes running green, and be ready to walk the screenshots if the live run misbehaves. Never let a failed live run derail the pitch — "here it is running earlier, let me show you the design" recovers gracefully.

11 Pitfalls to avoid

  • Presenting like an engineer. The number-one failure mode. Sell first, prove with the demo second.
  • Over-building. They told you: not production ready, content over format. A complex build presented flat loses to a simple one sold well.
  • Reading the legacy deck verbatim. It is dated reference material, not your script. Reframe it (§7) and layer 2026 on top (§8).
  • Skipping the queue answer. Requirement 5 is the trap that finds the real SC. Lead with it, do not wait to be asked.
  • Live-demo with no fallback. Have screenshots / a recording of green runs. (§6)
  • Invented Boomi features. Stick to what the platform really does. The 2026 features in §8 are real and public; do not improvise beyond them.
  • No assumptions stated. Silence on assumptions reads as you did not notice the gaps. State them (§5).
